1. Selling Property in India as an NRI: TAN Headache Removed

Quick Background: What is TDS and TAN?

TDS (Tax Deducted at Source): When someone buys property from an NRI, they must cut some tax from the amount they pay and deposit it with the government on the seller's behalf.

TAN (Tax Deduction and Collection Account Number): A special number separate from PAN, needed if a person is deducting TDS in many situations.

Old Rule (Before Budget 2026)

If a resident individual bought property from an NRI seller, the buyer had to:

  • Apply for a TAN (separate from PAN)
  • Deduct TDS from the sale consideration
  • Deposit that TDS using TAN
  • File TAN-based TDS returns and comply with all related rules

This created problems:

  • Most homebuyers only do property transactions once or twice in life, but still had to understand TAN, apply for it, and handle extra forms and deadlines
  • Many buyers were scared of "NRI seller = heavy compliance" and either delayed the deal or backed out to find a resident seller instead

New Rule After Budget 2026

‚úÖ Budget 2026 removes the TAN requirement for such buyers.

The buyer will use PAN only, deduct TDS at the same applicable rate as before, and deposit TDS via a simple PAN-based challan.

Important points:

  • TDS itself is still required. Only the procedure and compliance burden have been simplified.
  • TDS rates on NRI property sales are not reduced in this change.

Simple Example

Imagine an NRI is selling a flat in Bengaluru for ‚Çπ1.5 crore. The buyer is a salaried person based in India.

Before Budget 2026 After Budget 2026

Why This Matters to NRIs

  • Less friction in selling property
  • Fewer excuses from buyers like "NRI seller means too much headache"
  • Better chance of smoother transactions, timely payments, and proper TDS credit appearing in your Form 26AS

This is a process relief, not a tax rate benefit‚Äîbut in property deals, process pain is often as big as the tax itself.

2. One-Time 6-Month Window to Disclose Foreign Assets and Income

This is crucial for NRIs and returning NRIs who:

  • Opened foreign bank accounts as students
  • Received RSUs/ESOPs in foreign companies
  • Invested in foreign funds or insurance products
  • Are not 100% sure if everything was correctly disclosed in Indian tax returns

What Was the Earlier Fear?

India has a tough law: the Black Money (Undisclosed Foreign Income and Assets) Act.

If a person has foreign assets or income and fails to properly disclose them in Indian returns:

  • Heavy penalties
  • In extreme cases, prosecution (criminal cases)

This especially trapped:

  • Students and young professionals who forgot about small foreign accounts
  • Tech employees who did not fully understand disclosure rules for ESOPs/RSUs
  • Returning NRIs who assumed "foreign accounts are small, no one cares"

What Budget 2026 Introduces

üîî Budget 2026 provides a one-time, 6-month disclosure window for foreign income and assets.

Category A: Undisclosed Foreign Income & Assets up to ‚Çπ1 Crore

  • Case: The foreign asset or income was never disclosed at all in any return
  • Condition: Total value up to ‚Çπ1 crore
  • Cost to regularise: 30% tax + 30% penalty = 60% total outgo
  • Benefit: Full immunity from prosecution under the Black Money Act

Category B: Asset Not Reported, but Income Already Taxed (up to ‚Çπ5 Crore)

  • Case: The income from the asset was included in the Indian return, but the asset itself was not shown in Schedule FA (foreign asset schedule)
  • Example: Foreign salary was taxed in India, but the foreign bank account holding that salary balance was not reported
  • Limit: Total foreign assets up to ‚Çπ5 crore
  • Cost: ‚Çπ1 lakh flat fee
  • Benefit: Relief from penalty and prosecution for this kind of non-reporting

Relief for Very Small Foreign Assets

Very small foreign movable assets (non-immovable) up to around ‚Çπ20 lakh are effectively immune from prosecution, recognising that small balances are often missed accidentally.

Who Should Seriously Look at This?

  • Returning NRIs with old foreign bank accounts that were never declared
  • Residents who earlier worked abroad and have small brokerage accounts, ESOP/RSU holdings, or dormant foreign accounts
  • Anyone who realises: "There is something abroad; it is not properly shown in Schedule FA; better to clean it up while there is an official window"

This scheme is not cheap (especially Category A), but it removes the fear of future Black Money Act action, which can be far more painful.

3. 5-Year Tax Exemption on Foreign Income for Certain Returning/Visiting NRIs

Normal Residency Rule (Super Simplified)

Under regular rules, if a person stays in India for 182 days or more in a financial year, they may become a resident for tax purposes. Once resident, India can tax global income (not just Indian income).

For NRIs who work across countries, this is scary: taking a long assignment in India could mean salary from foreign employers, overseas interest/dividends, all become taxable in India too.

What Budget 2026 Does

üåç Special carve-out for certain skilled NRIs and foreign professionals:

For 5 years from the date of taking up work in India, their foreign income will be exempt from Indian tax, even if they satisfy residency conditions.

Conditions (Broadly):

  • The person should have been non-resident for at least 5 continuous years before coming to India
  • They come to India to work under a government-notified scheme, project, or sector (likely high-tech, strategic, or priority areas)

Important Clarifications:

  • This is not a blanket benefit for every NRI who comes back
  • Applies only to people working on specific, notified projects/sectors
  • Only foreign income gets exempt; income sourced in India (salary paid in India, rent from Indian property, interest from NRO, etc.) will still be taxable

Why This is Important for NRIs

Encourages highly skilled NRIs to take up longer projects in India and contribute expertise here‚Äîwithout fear that all foreign savings and income will suddenly face Indian tax.

4. NRIs and Other Overseas Individuals Can Own More of Indian Companies

Some Terminology

  • NRI / OCI: Non-Resident Indian / Overseas Citizen of India
  • PROI (Person Resident Outside India): Anyone who is not a resident in India ‚Äì includes NRIs, OCIs, and foreign nationals
  • PIS (Portfolio Investment Scheme): A route for NRIs to directly buy and sell shares of Indian companies through designated banks

Old Rules (Simplified)

For investments via the NRI portfolio route:

  • Individual limit for one NRI in one company: Typically 5% of the paid-up capital
  • Aggregate limit for all NRIs/OCIs together: Default 10% (could be raised to 24% by shareholder approval)

New Rules in Budget 2026

Limit Type Before After Budget 2026
Individual limit per company 5% 10%
Aggregate limit (all non-residents) 10% (default) 24% (default)

Additional changes:

  • PIS-like portfolio investment access is now more clearly available not only to NRIs/OCIs but to wider PROIs
  • PROIs can more easily access Portfolio Management Services (PMS) in India without always needing complex FPI registrations

What This Means in Plain Language

NRIs who like to pick Indian stocks can build larger positions in favourite companies before hitting regulatory caps. This signals that India wants deeper participation from the diaspora and global investors‚Äîmore capital into Indian markets, but via clean, regulated channels.

5. New Income Tax Act 2025: Clearer "Special Regime" for NRIs

Big Picture: A New Tax Law

India is replacing the old Income-tax Act, 1961 with a brand-new Income Tax Act, 2025, which will apply from 1 April 2026 (i.e., from FY 2026‚Äì27).

NRIs are directly affected because the new Act reorganises all the special rules, rates, and benefits applicable to non-residents.

What Changes for NRIs?

  • There is a "Special Tax Regime" for NRIs that was earlier scattered across many sections; now it is consolidated and made more systematic
  • Budget 2026 mainly focuses on clarity and simplification, not on slashing rates
  • Tries to reduce disputes by writing clearer definitions and rules

From FY 2026‚Äì27, NRIs do not necessarily get lower tax rates, but the rules become more organised and easier to understand.

6. More Time to Fix Mistakes in Tax Returns (Including NRI Returns)

Old Timelines

  • Filing due date: Most salaried and simple-income taxpayers: 31 July
  • Revised return deadline: Up to 31 December of the same assessment year

For NRIs, this was tight because they may wait for foreign tax residency certificates, final foreign tax documents, and may discover late that Schedule FA is incomplete or foreign tax credit computations need correction.

New Rules in Budget 2026

üìÖ Revised return deadline extended from 31 December to 31 March of the assessment year.

A small fee (e.g., ‚Çπ1,000 or ‚Çπ5,000 based on income) applies if filed after 31 December‚Äîbut it's still far better than being stuck with a wrong original return.

Why This Helps NRIs

  • More time to collect foreign documents and reconcile global income
  • Correct missing or wrong details in Schedule FA
  • Properly claim foreign tax credit once foreign returns are finalised

For many NRIs, life is spread across time zones and financial years; this extra 3-month buffer can make the difference between smooth compliance vs chronic errors and notices.
