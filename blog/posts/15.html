What exactly is FATCA and why does every bank ask for it?

FATCA stands for Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act and CRS stands for Common Reporting Standard. These are international information-sharing regulations aimed at preventing tax evasion.

üá∫üá∏ What is FATCA?

FATCA is a U.S. law (enacted in 2010) that requires financial institutions around the world to report accounts held by U.S. citizens or residents to the U.S. IRS. If you're a U.S. Person, FATCA wants to know if you have money in an Indian bank.

üåç What is CRS?

CRS is a global standard by the OECD. Over 100 countries have adopted CRS. Under CRS, financial institutions identify customers' tax residencies and report financial account details to relevant jurisdictions. It's like a multilateral version of FATCA.

Why banks ask: Banks must collect information about your tax residency to comply with reporting obligations. Each year, they report account details of foreign residents to local tax authorities.

üìã What You Need to Provide

When opening an account as an NRI, you'll fill a FATCA/CRS declaration form asking:

  • Are you a tax resident of any country other than India?
  • If yes, provide country name and Tax ID number

As a UAE NRI, you'll declare UAE as your country of tax residence (even though UAE has no income tax, for CRS purposes you are still a resident of UAE).
